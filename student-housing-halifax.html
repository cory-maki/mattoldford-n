Halifax's Student Housing Gap — and How Purpose-Built Supply Fills It

📅 May 2025
✎ Matthew Oldford
⌚ 7 min read

Halifax is home to five universities and a college, tens of thousands of students, and a rental market that simply hasn't kept pace. The result is a structural housing problem that affects not just students, but the entire city's rental ecosystem. Matthew Oldford is building part of the answer.

Halifax's student population isn't a temporary variable — it's a permanent feature of the city's economy and culture. Dalhousie, Saint Mary's, NSCC, the Nova Scotia College of Art and Design, and several other institutions draw students from across Canada and internationally every year. Most of them need housing. Most of the housing they find was never designed for them.

The Structural Problem

When students compete for general rental housing in a tight market, prices rise and availability shrinks for everyone. Families, young professionals, and long-term residents all feel the squeeze that results from students filling units never designed with them in mind. The problem isn't that students exist — it's that Halifax has chronically underdeveloped purpose-built housing for them. When students live in units designed for families, both groups lose.

Purpose-built student housing solves this by creating a dedicated, efficiently designed supply. Smaller units optimized for one or two occupants, within walking distance of campuses, with the amenities students actually need — these buildings serve a specific market and free up larger family units for the people they were built for. It's a supply intervention that benefits the whole rental ecosystem, not just the students who live there. Matthew Oldford has written about related community and housing themes on his Medium publication.

"Purpose-built student housing doesn't just solve a student problem. It releases pressure on every other rental category in the city."

Why the South End

Halifax's South End is the most logical location for this kind of development. It's walking distance from Dalhousie and several other institutions. It has existing transit connections. It's a neighbourhood students already want to live in — the question has always been whether quality, purpose-designed supply would exist there at scale.

Matthew Oldford's two new student housing buildings in the South End are designed with exactly that logic. These aren't converted single-family homes or repurposed commercial spaces — they're built from the ground up to serve students well, with efficient layouts, durable finishes, and thoughtful design that won't deteriorate within a few rental cycles. Oldford's background in construction means he knows what holds up over time and what doesn't, which matters enormously in the student housing sector where wear is accelerated.

The Development Lens

From a development economics standpoint, purpose-built student housing in Halifax makes strong sense right now. Demand is structurally reliable — students will come every September regardless of broader economic conditions. Vacancy risk is lower than general rental. And the gap between current supply and what the market needs creates a window that won't stay open indefinitely as more developers recognize it.

Oldford approaches these projects the same way he approaches every development: with rigorous financial modelling up front and construction discipline throughout. His background — a decade in construction trades, five years in financial planning and mortgage work, years as a LIUNA foreman — gives him an unusual ability to evaluate these opportunities from both ends simultaneously. Community Impact as a Design Criterion

Beyond the economics, Oldford cares about what his buildings contribute to the neighbourhoods they're in. Quality construction matters — not just for durability and returns, but because buildings that are well built become assets to a street, not liabilities. Poorly constructed rental housing drags down the blocks around it. Good development lifts them.

This is a philosophy he's carried since founding Matty's Renos in 2018: treat every project as a long-term investment, not a short-term transaction. The evolution from renovation work to multi-unit development has amplified that commitment, not diluted it. Buildings that serve the people who live in them, and the communities around them, are what Matthew Oldford is building in Halifax.

Conclusion

Halifax's student housing gap is a structural problem with a structural solution: purpose-built supply in the right locations, designed and built to a standard that serves both tenants and the broader rental market. Matthew Oldford is building that supply in Halifax's South End — two projects that reflect a long-term investment in the city's housing future.
