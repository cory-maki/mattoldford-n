Why Finance and Construction Need to Be in the Same Brain

📅 June 2025
✎ Matthew Oldford
⌚ 6 min read

Most developers come from one of two places: they were builders who learned money the hard way, or investors who hired builders and hoped for the best. Matthew Oldford spent years occupying both worlds — deliberately — and the difference it makes is fundamental.

There's a gap in real estate development that rarely gets talked about honestly. It's the space between what a building costs to build and what it's worth when it's done — and in that gap lives most of the decision-making that determines whether a project succeeds or fails. Fill it with good data and experienced judgment, and development is a disciplined craft. Fill it with guesses and optimism, and it becomes expensive gambling.

Two Educations, One Career

Matthew Oldford's path to development wasn't linear — it was intentional. He entered the construction trades at 22, worked five years in coastal roofing and project management, and then did something most builders don't: he returned to school to study finance. Completing the Canadian Securities Course, the Life Licence Qualification Program, and financial planning credentials through NSCC, he then spent five years as a financial planner and mortgage specialist with Scotiabank.

That detour — which wasn't really a detour at all — gave him a vocabulary that most builders lack. He learned how investors evaluate assets, how banks assess risk, how cash flow is modelled, and how long-term value is built through disciplined capital allocation. When he returned to construction as a LIUNA foreman and later founded Matty's Renos, he brought that lens with him.

"Every renovation budget is a financial forecast. Every project timeline is a cash-flow schedule. If you can't read them both, you're operating blind."

What Financial Training Changes on a Job Site

The practical impact shows up in how Oldford approaches every project. When evaluating a renovation or conversion opportunity in Halifax, he isn't just asking what it will cost to build — he's asking what the market will bear, what the capitalization rate looks like, how long the carry costs run, and what the exit or refinance scenario looks like two years out. These aren't questions most contractors ask. They are, however, the questions that determine whether a project actually creates wealth or just creates noise.

He's also seen the opposite — investors who buy properties based on projected returns, then get blindsided by construction realities they didn't model correctly. Scope creep. Structural surprises behind walls. Subcontractor delays. Material escalation. Each of these has a dollar value, and experienced builders know how to price for them. The investor who doesn't understand the build side ends up carrying costs they never budgeted for. Read more about Matthew Oldford's approach to development on his YouTube channel.

Applying the Model to Halifax Development

Halifax is at an interesting inflection point. The city is growing faster than its housing stock, rental vacancy is tight, and purpose-built supply — particularly for students and young professionals — is structurally short. This creates opportunity for developers who can move quickly and deliver quality product at viable economics.

That's exactly the lens Oldford is applying to his 17-unit building on Prince Albert Road and the two purpose-built student housing developments in Halifax's South End. These aren't speculative flips. They're long-hold investments designed from the ground up — financially modelled before the first shovel, then built with the cost discipline of someone who spent years on the tools. The combination is rare, and it shows in the numbers.

The Lesson for Aspiring Developers

For people earlier in their careers — whether they're coming from trades or from finance — Oldford's advice is direct: learn the other side. If you're a builder, take a course in real estate finance. Understand cap rates, debt service coverage, and how to read a pro forma. If you're an investor, spend time on actual job sites. Understand what labour costs, what materials do, what timeline delays really mean.

The developers who consistently outperform in markets like Halifax are the ones who can hold both conversations at once. They're not dependent on advisors to translate between disciplines — they understand both, which means faster decisions, fewer surprises, and better outcomes for everyone involved. Conclusion

The best developers aren't builders who got lucky with money, or investors who hired good builders. They're people who've made a real effort to understand both sides of the ledger. Matthew Oldford's career is a working argument for that model — and the portfolio he's building in Halifax is the evidence.
